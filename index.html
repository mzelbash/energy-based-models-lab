What is an energy-based model, and why should you care?

Read this page first. By the end of it you will understand the core idea, even if you have never heard the term before. The rest of the app then lets you build, sample from, and train one, step by step, in your browser.

1. What it is

An energy-based model learns a single function, the energy E(x), that scores any input with one number. Low energy means plausible (looks like real data), high energy means implausible. That is the whole idea.

2. Why we need it

Instead of forcing a model to output a proper probability, which needs an impossible normalizing sum, we only learn a relative score. This lets the model represent very flexible distributions, and it turns "is this realistic?" into "what is the energy?".

3. What you will do

You will watch random noise turn into a handwritten digit by rolling downhill on the energy surface, then train the energy function yourself and watch the samples sharpen.

Key framing

An energy-based model is a framework, not one specific model. Many models are energy-based models underneath: the deep network you will build here, the classic Boltzmann machine and restricted Boltzmann machine, and even an ordinary image classifier. They all define an energy over their inputs. Once you see the pattern, you see it everywhere.

The idea

Energy, scores, and the Boltzmann distribution

Physics gives us a clean way to turn any energy score into a probability. That single equation is the foundation of everything that follows.

A short story, the running coach

Diane Mixx could judge a runner just by sensing their leftover energy after a race. Elite runners finished with very little energy left, they gave everything. To turn an average runner into an elite one, she measured their current energy, worked out the small adjustment that would lower it next time, and repeated. Over many small steps, an ordinary runner became indistinguishable from a champion.

That is exactly an energy-based model. The energy function judges how realistic something is, and a sampler makes many small downhill adjustments until noise becomes realistic.

The Boltzmann distribution

We convert an energy E(x) into a probability with the Boltzmann distribution. Low energy gives high probability, high energy gives low probability.

p(x)= e−E(x) ∫e−E(x^)dx^

The bottom is the tricky part: a sum over every possible image. It keeps the total probability at 1, but it is impossible to compute for real problems. The clever trick of energy-based models is that, using the right training and sampling methods, we never need to compute it.

Try it, energy to probability

Drag the sliders to set the energy of four candidate images. Watch how the Boltzmann distribution converts these energies into probabilities. Notice that only differences in energy matter, and the lowest energy always wins the largest probability.

The two hard problems, and the two tricks

Problem one: how do we generate a low energy image, not just score one? Trick: Langevin dynamics. Problem two: the normalizing sum is intractable, so we cannot train by maximum likelihood directly. Trick: contrastive divergence. You will meet both, with live demos.

Step 1

The energy function E(x)

The energy function is just a neural network that reads an image and returns one number. It is built from convolutional layers that shrink the image while growing the channel count.

The architecture

Four stride 2 convolutions take the 32 by 32 image down to 2 by 2, then two dense layers reduce it to a single score. The last layer is linear, so the output can be any real number.

The swish activation

swish(x)=x·σ(x)= xe−x+1
swish is smooth, unlike ReLU

Smoothness matters because the sampler takes gradients of this network with respect to the input. A kinked function like ReLU gives unstable gradients.

Step 2, sampling

Langevin dynamics, turning noise into a digit

The energy function only scores an image. To generate one, we start from noise and take many small steps downhill on the energy, adding a little randomness each time.

xk= xk−1 −η∇xE(xk−1) +ω

Read it in three parts: start from the current image, step a small amount η in the direction that lowers the energy, then add a pinch of noise ω so the sampler does not get stuck. Repeat.

Why the noise matters

Turn the noise slider to zero and run again. The sampler becomes a plain gradient descent that can settle into a poor local dip. A little noise lets it escape and explore, which is why this is called stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics.

Step 3, training

Contrastive divergence, training the energy

We cannot train by maximum likelihood because of that intractable sum. Instead we contrast real images against fake ones from the sampler, and push their energies apart.

The loss in one line

loss=E(real)−E(fake)+reg

Minimizing this pushes the energy of real digits down and the energy of the sampler's fakes up. The small regularizer keeps the raw scores from drifting too far. No normalizing sum appears anywhere.

One step of contrastive divergence

Real observations are pressed down into the energy valley, fake observations are lifted up out of it. After many steps the valleys sit exactly where the real data lives.

Context

Other energy-based models

The deep EBM you built is a modern member of an old family. Seeing an earlier one, the restricted Boltzmann machine, makes clear why Langevin sampling was such a step forward.

The Boltzmann machine

E(v,h)=− 12 (vTLv+ hTJh+ vTWh)

Binary visible units v and hidden units h, with learned weight matrices. A restricted Boltzmann machine removes the within layer connections, leaving a clean two layer bipartite graph that can be sampled by alternating updates.

Live restricted Boltzmann machine

A tiny RBM trained on the digits. Start from a real digit, hide half of it, and watch Gibbs sampling alternate between hidden and visible units to reconstruct the image. Notice how many steps it takes, this slow mixing is exactly why deep EBMs prefer Langevin dynamics.

Where this leads

Langevin sampling of deep EBMs led to a training idea called score matching, which in turn grew into denoising diffusion models, the engine behind modern image generators. The energy view is a thread that runs from 1985 Boltzmann machines all the way to today's diffusion models.

A surprising connection

Your classifier is secretly an EBM

Energy-based models are not only for generation. A plain image classifier, trained only to label digits, already contains an energy over its inputs. Here is the one line that reveals it.

From logits to energy

A classifier outputs ten logits f(x). The softmax of those logits is the usual class probability. But the same logits also define an energy over the input:

E(x)=−log ∑y efy(x)

This is the negative log sum exp of the logits. If the network is confident that an input looks like some digit, the energy is low. If no class fits, the energy is high. So a discriminative model is an energy-based model in disguise.

Try it with a real classifier

We trained an ordinary digit classifier (no energy tricks) to 98 percent accuracy. Now we read its logits as an energy and compare real digits against random noise, averaged over many samples of each. Real digits sit at clearly lower energy. We average because a plain classifier can be overconfident on a single noise image, which is exactly the calibration issue that energy aware training later fixes.

Why this is useful

Because the classifier carries an energy, you can use it to detect inputs that do not look like any training digit (they get high energy), and in principle even generate digits with the same Langevin sampler you used on the deep EBM. One network, two readings, discriminative and generative.
