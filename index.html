What is an energy-based model, and why should you care?

Read this page first. By the end of it you will understand the core idea, even if you have never heard the term before. The rest of the app then lets you build, sample from, and train one, step by step, in your browser.

1. What it is

An energy-based model learns a single function, the energy E(x), that scores any input with one number. Low energy means plausible (looks like real data), high energy means implausible. That is the whole idea.

2. Why we need it

Instead of forcing a model to output a proper probability, which needs an impossible normalizing sum, we only learn a relative score. This lets the model represent very flexible distributions, and it turns "is this realistic?" into "what is the energy?".

3. What you will do

You will watch random noise turn into a handwritten digit by rolling downhill on the energy surface, then train the energy function yourself and watch the samples sharpen.

Key framing

An energy-based model is a framework, not one specific model. Many models are energy-based models underneath: the deep network you will build here, the classic Boltzmann machine and restricted Boltzmann machine, and even an ordinary image classifier. They all define an energy over their inputs. Once you see the pattern, you see it everywhere.

The idea

Energy, scores, and the Boltzmann distribution

Physics gives us a clean way to turn any energy score into a probability. That single equation is the foundation of everything that follows.

A short story, the running coach

Diane Mixx could judge a runner just by sensing their leftover energy after a race. Elite runners finished with very little energy left, they gave everything. To turn an average runner into an elite one, she measured their current energy, worked out the small adjustment that would lower it next time, and repeated. Over many small steps, an ordinary runner became indistinguishable from a champion.

That is exactly an energy-based model. The energy function judges how realistic something is, and a sampler makes many small downhill adjustments until noise becomes realistic.

The Boltzmann distribution

We convert an energy E(x) into a probability with the Boltzmann distribution. Low energy gives high probability, high energy gives low probability.

p(x)= e−E(x) ∫e−E(x^)dx^

The bottom is the tricky part: a sum over every possible image. It keeps the total probability at 1, but it is impossible to compute for real problems. The clever trick of energy-based models is that, using the right training and sampling methods, we never need to compute it.

Try it, energy to probability

Drag the sliders to set the energy of four candidate images. Watch how the Boltzmann distribution converts these energies into probabilities. Notice that only differences in energy matter, and the lowest energy always wins the largest probability.

The two hard problems, and the two tricks

Problem one: how do we generate a low energy image, not just score one? Trick: Langevin dynamics. Problem two: the normalizing sum is intractable, so we cannot train by maximum likelihood directly. Trick: contrastive divergence. You will meet both, with live demos.

Step 1

The energy function E(x)

The energy function is just a neural network that reads an image and returns one number. It is built from convolutional layers that shrink the image while growing the channel count.

The architecture

Four stride 2 convolutions take the 32 by 32 image down to 2 by 2, then two dense layers reduce it to a single score. The last layer is linear, so the output can be any real number.

The swish activation

swish(x)=x·σ(x)= xe−x+1
swish is smooth, unlike ReLU

Smoothness matters because the sampler takes gradients of this network with respect to the input. A kinked function like ReLU gives unstable gradients.

Step 2, sampling

Langevin dynamics, turning noise into a digit

The energy function only scores an image. To generate one, we start from noise and take many small steps downhill on the energy, adding a little randomness each time.

xk= xk−1 −η∇xE(xk−1) +ω

Read it in three parts: start from the current image, step a small amount η in the direction that lowers the energy, then add a pinch of noise ω so the sampler does not get stuck. Repeat.

Why the noise matters

Turn the noise slider to zero and run again. The sampler becomes a plain gradient descent that can settle into a poor local dip. A little noise lets it escape and explore, which is why this is called stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics.

Step 3, training

Contrastive divergence, training the energy

We cannot train by maximum likelihood because of that intractable sum. Instead we contrast real images against fake ones from the sampler, and push their energies apart.

The loss in one line

loss=E(real)−E(fake)+reg

Minimizing this pushes the energy of real digits down and the energy of the sampler's fakes up. The small regularizer keeps the raw scores from drifting too far. No normalizing sum appears anywhere.

One step of contrastive divergence

Real observations are pressed down into the energy valley, fake observations are lifted up out of it. After many steps the valleys sit exactly where the real data lives.

The buffer, live fakes

These are the current fake samples the training loop is contrasting against. Early on they are noise. As training proceeds they should start to look like digits.

What is the replay buffer for?

Every training step needs fake samples to push the energy up on. Producing a good fake means running the Langevin sampler for many steps, which is expensive. If we started each fake from pure noise every step, we would waste that work and training would crawl.

The replay buffer fixes this. It keeps a pool of the fakes produced so far, and each step it mostly reuses them (with only about 5 percent freshly seeded from noise) and pushes them a few more Langevin steps. So the fakes improve gradually across steps instead of restarting, which makes training much faster and gives a more stable contrast between real and fake. Watch the samples above sharpen from noise into digit shapes as the buffer matures.

Context and history

The restricted Boltzmann machine

The deep EBM we built is a modern member of an old family. The restricted Boltzmann machine (RBM) is one of the earliest energy-based models. It is worth understanding because it makes the energy idea concrete: a few units, some weights, one energy number, and a simple way to sample.

The building blocks

Visible units, v

The visible units hold the data you observe. For an MNIST image, each visible unit is one pixel, usually binary: 0 for a dark or inactive pixel, 1 for a bright or active pixel.

v1, v2, ... v1024 for a 32 by 32 image. They are called visible because their values come straight from the data.

Hidden units, h

The hidden units are not given by the data. They learn patterns in the visible pixels, for example a vertical line, a curve, a loop, or the top of a digit: combinations of pixels that often occur together.

h1, h2, ... Traditionally the hidden units are also binary, on or off.

Learned weights, wij

Every visible unit connects to every hidden unit through a learned weight that says how compatible the two units are.

  • Large positive weight: the two like to be active together.
  • Large negative weight: they discourage each other.
  • Near zero: little relationship.

The model also learns a bias for every visible and hidden unit.

What does "restricted" mean?

A full Boltzmann machine allows every kind of connection: visible to visible, hidden to hidden, and visible to hidden. That creates many tangled dependencies and makes sampling slow. A restricted Boltzmann machine keeps only the visible to hidden connections and removes the two within-layer kinds.

Full Boltzmann machine

Connections everywhere, including within each layer. Powerful, but the tangled dependencies make it very slow to train and sample.

Restricted (RBM)

Only visible to hidden connections remain. This two-layer structure with no within-layer links is called a bipartite graph.

Bipartite means two groups

Connections are allowed only between the visible group and the hidden group, never inside a group. Visible to hidden is allowed. Visible to visible and hidden to hidden are not. That single restriction is what makes the RBM fast to sample.

The energy of a configuration

Every energy-based model assigns one energy number to a configuration, and low energy means likely.

General Boltzmann machine

E(v,h)=− 12 (vTLv+ hTJh+ vTWh)

Three interaction terms:

  • vᵀLv: visible to visible interactions (matrix L).
  • hᵀJh: hidden to hidden interactions (matrix J).
  • vᵀWh: visible to hidden coupling (matrix W).

The RBM energy

E(v,h)=−( vTWh+ aTv+ bTh)

Because an RBM has no within-layer connections, the L and J terms vanish. Only the visible to hidden coupling W and the biases a and b remain. This is the exact energy the live demo below uses.

Sampling by alternating updates

What is Gibbs sampling?

Gibbs sampling is the procedure an RBM uses to move back and forth between the visible layer and the hidden layer. Starting from the current image it samples the hidden units, then from those hidden units it samples a fresh image, then the hidden units again, and so on. Each back and forth pass is one Gibbs step, and repeating it gradually turns a rough or partial image into a clean, plausible one.

Why alternation works

Because there are no connections between hidden units, once the visible units are known, every hidden unit can be computed independently. And because there are no connections between visible units, once the hidden units are known, every visible unit can be computed independently.

So the model updates a whole layer at a time, alternating back and forth:

v → h → v → h → v

Each unit turns on with a probability given by a sigmoid of its inputs:

p(hj=1∣v)= σ(bj+ ∑iviwij)
p(vi=1∣h)= σ(ai+ ∑jwijhj)

This back and forth is Gibbs sampling. Each pass moves the network toward a configuration that fits its learned energy. For MNIST, visible pixels drive hidden stroke and curve detectors, which in turn regenerate likely pixels, reconstructing a digit.

Where this leads

Langevin sampling of deep EBMs led to a training idea called score matching, which in turn grew into denoising diffusion models, the engine behind modern image generators. The energy view is a thread that runs from the 1985 Boltzmann machine all the way to today's diffusion models.

A surprising connection

Your classifier is secretly an EBM

Energy-based models are not only for generation. A plain image classifier, trained only to label digits, already contains an energy over its inputs. Here is the one line that reveals it.

From logits to energy

A classifier outputs ten logits f(x). The softmax of those logits is the usual class probability. But the same logits also define an energy over the input:

E(x)=−log ∑y efy(x)

This is the negative log sum exp of the logits. If the network is confident that an input looks like some digit, the energy is low. If no class fits, the energy is high. So a discriminative model is an energy-based model in disguise.

We trained an ordinary digit classifier (no energy tricks) to 98 percent accuracy. We average because a plain classifier can be overconfident on a single noise image, which is exactly the calibration issue that energy aware training later fixes.

Worked example: from logits to one energy number

Let us follow the exact arithmetic on a single image. Suppose the classifier looks at an image and outputs these ten logits f(x):

digit 01 digit 12 digit 28 digits 3 to 90

Computing the energy, E(x) = −log∑ef(x)

1

Exponentiate every logit. e1 ≈ 2.72, e2 ≈ 7.39, e8 ≈ 2980.96, e0 = 1. The seven digits with logit 0 contribute 7 × 1 = 7.

2

Add them together (this is the sum-exp part): 2.72 + 7.39 + 2980.96 + 7 ≈ 2998.07.

3

Take the logarithm: log(2998.07) ≈ 8.006. Notice it lands very close to the largest logit, 8. That is why log-sum-exp is called a smooth maximum.

4

Add the negative sign: E(x) ≈ -8.006. One very strong logit gives this image a low energy, so the classifier finds it very plausible.

The same logits as softmax probabilities

Divide each exponentiated logit by the sum 2998.07:

Digit 2
99.43%
Digit 1
0.25%
Digit 0
0.09%
Each of 3 to 9
0.03%

So the same numbers give two readings:

  • Softmax asks which digit is most likely. Answer: digit 2.
  • Energy asks how strong the total evidence is for this image. Answer: very strong, so the energy is low (about -8).
The takeaway

The energy is not based only on the winning class, it uses every logit. But because logit 8 dominates the others, the log-sum-exp ends up very close to 8, and the energy ends up very close to -8. A confident classifier assigns low energy, an uncertain one assigns high energy.

Why this is useful

Because the classifier carries an energy, you can use it to detect inputs that do not look like any training digit (they get high energy), and in principle even generate digits with the same Langevin sampler you used on the deep EBM. One network, two readings, discriminative and generative.
